    Problem Statement

    Families record more than ever, yet remember less. Photos, videos, voice notes, and journals accumulate across phones, laptops, clouds, and apps‚Äînone designed to preserve a coherent family history over decades. The result is drift: fragments without story, accounts that expire, formats that age out, and moments that become impossible to retrieve or contextualize.

    Fragmentation & loss. Memories live in silos with different logins, billing cycles, and failure modes. Devices break, subscriptions lapse, links rot, and platforms change terms or shut down. Even when raw files survive, families often cannot find what matters when they need it.

    Missing context. A photo of a birthday cake is not the memory. Without who/where/why‚Äîthe voices, relationships, emotions, and small details‚Äîarchives degrade into unlabeled media. Future generations inherit puzzles instead of stories.

    Trust & privacy deficit. General-purpose platforms are optimized for engagement and advertising, not family stewardship. Data mining, opaque AI training, and shifting privacy policies undermine confidence. Parents, in particular, lack a safe place for children‚Äôs milestones and voices.

    Longevity & portability. Proprietary formats, closed ecosystems, and weak export paths make long-term custody uncertain. Families need durability measured in decades, not product cycles, plus clear posthumous access and inheritance controls.

    Family workflows, not single-user tools. Most apps assume one owner. Real families need roles, permissions, and shared contribution across parents, kids, and grandparents‚Äîalong with guardrails for sensitive content and age-appropriate access.

    Friction at capture time. Tools that require perfect organization habits fail in real life. Without low-effort daily capture (especially voice) and simple retrieval, families stop recording‚Äîand memories fade in the gap.

    Why now. The volume of personal media is exploding while institutional trust declines. An aging population wants to pass down stories; younger families expect privacy by default. AI raises expectations for search and recall but also raises the bar for provenance and consent. Without a purpose-built, privacy-first system, the window to preserve authentic voices and narratives will continue to close.

    In short: memories are fragile; tools are fragmented; trust is thin; longevity is uncertain; and families lack a coherent, multi-user way to capture and keep context. Without solving these fundamentals, the next generation won‚Äôt inherit a living record‚Äîthey‚Äôll inherit a disorganized pile of files.

    Technical Architecture

    Heirloom is built as a modular, privacy-first SaaS with a simple goal: make daily capture effortless today while guaranteeing families can still access their memories decades from now. The near-term system is a cloud-hosted, multi-tenant application with clear domain boundaries (capture, archive, search/recall, billing, and admin). We start with a ‚Äúmodular monolith‚Äù codebase‚Äîone deployable unit with well-defined modules‚Äîto reduce operational complexity early, then peel off services as load and team size grow.

    At the center is a unified archive. Entries‚Äîtext, voice, short video, and photos‚Äîare stored as immutable records with rich metadata: who captured them, when, where, and how they relate to people and events. Media files are kept in durable object storage; structured metadata lives in a relational store for integrity and transactional updates. Every asset carries a content hash for integrity checks and de-duplication. Families can tag relationships (people, places, milestones) without breaking the underlying immutability, which means narratives can evolve while the source artifacts remain stable.

    The capture pipeline is optimized for low friction. Mobile and web clients enqueue captures immediately and upload in the background, so users aren‚Äôt blocked by network conditions. Media is transcoded into web-safe formats and multiple resolutions on ingest to balance quality with cost. Voice entries are optionally transcribed; transcripts and captions are attached as first-class fields so they‚Äôre searchable without touching the raw files. All write paths are idempotent to survive flaky networks and retries.

    Search and recall run on a blended index. Exact metadata queries use the relational store. Free-text search is powered by an inverted index over transcripts, captions, and journals. Natural-language recall is layered on top: we derive compact embeddings from family-owned content and maintain a private vector index per family. The retrieval flow first resolves permissions, then fans out to metadata, full-text, and vector stores, finally composing results back into a single timeline. AI narratives are generated on demand from the family‚Äôs own data with strict scoping to that family‚Äôs vault.

    Privacy boundaries are enforced in code and in cryptography. All transport uses TLS 1.3; storage is encrypted at rest with envelope keys from a managed key service. Access to keys and media paths is mediated by short-lived tokens and least-privilege roles. For highly sensitive material, families can opt into client-side encryption vaults: content is encrypted before upload and remains opaque to the server; only encrypted blobs and metadata envelopes are stored. Recovery flows are explicit and documented so families don‚Äôt inadvertently lock themselves out.

    Identity and permissions are designed for real households. Each archive has owners, guardians, and contributors, with per-entry privacy controls and age-appropriate defaults. Invitations establish trust links between accounts; every read/write is authorized against both role and entry scope. Audit logs record sensitive operations (invites, permission changes, exports, deletions) so families can see who did what and when. Devices and sessions are visible and revocable.

    Reliability is engineered in from the start. The system targets tight recovery objectives: frequent point-in-time backups for databases, multi-region replication for object storage, and periodic restore drills to validate that exports remain usable. Background jobs are idempotent and observable; if a job fails mid-way (e.g., during a large import), it resumes without duplicating work. Rate limits and backpressure protect the system during spikes such as holidays or reunions when families upload in bursts.

    Cost control is part of the architecture. Media processing and AI features scale with queues and autosized workers; we batch transcription and embedding jobs to keep unit economics predictable. Cold media tiers and lifecycle rules automatically move rarely accessed assets to lower-cost storage while keeping previews and transcripts hot for fast recall. Telemetry focuses on the metrics that matter‚Äîcapture success rate, indexing latency, recall time‚Äîwithout collecting extraneous personal data.

    HeirloomOS is the path to local custody. When families are ready, their cloud archive can migrate to an in-home appliance that mirrors the same data model and APIs. The device ships with local-first encrypted storage, on-device search and narrative models, optional LAN-only access, and a one-click sync/unsync with the cloud. Data portability is symmetrical: families can export at any time in open formats (JSON/CSV for metadata, standard media containers for files) and verify exports via hashes.

    Operations favor prevention and transparency. Threat modeling and secure code reviews are mandatory for modules that touch auth, crypto, and storage. Dependencies are scanned continuously; third-party penetration tests are scheduled on a regular cadence. Incidents follow a defined runbook with containment, communication, and post-mortems. Sub-processors are listed publicly, and all data processing follows the same lifecycle rules as core storage, including deletions that propagate to backups on a documented schedule.

    In sum, the architecture is deliberately boring in the right places‚Äîdurable storage, strong keys, predictable queues‚Äîand ambitious where it matters to families: unified capture, fast recall, exportable narratives, and a credible path from SaaS convenience to local, long-term custody.
